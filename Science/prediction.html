The Complexity
of Prediction.

I have always liked the idea of predictability. It probably started with dice: the hope that doing the same thing again would produce the same result. Later it moved through probability, Newton, gravity, calculus, chaos, and finally quantum mechanics — places where prediction becomes less obvious and much more interesting.

01 · dice

I used to think prediction was repetition.

I remember trying to throw a dice in what felt like the same way.

Same direction. Same strength. Same movement.

6. Then 3. Then something else.

The movement looked almost identical. The result was not.

I started to think that maybe prediction was not about repeating everything. Maybe there was another layer underneath.
02 · probability

Randomness can still have structure.

Later I learned probability. That changed the way I looked at the dice.

Repeating the motion does not change the probability of an event. But we can still predict what randomness can give us over time.

A dice has six sides. Stable rules. Known possibilities.

probability
P(event) = successful possibilitiesall possibilities

The probability of rolling a 3 is:

one dice
P(3) = 1 successful result6 possible results

The exact result is uncertain. But the structure behind the result is predictable.

That idea was great to me. Now we could start playing with randomness instead of only being surprised by it.

03 · two dice

The pattern becomes visible.

Take two dice. Now we are not only looking at one face. We are looking at the sum of two results.

I still do not know the next result. But I can see what is more likely.

04 · Newton

Then physics enters the conversation.

Later I heard about Isaac Newton, gravity, and the apple story.

The idea was powerful: an apple falling from a tree is not only something we observe. It is something we can predict.

There is a constant pull on Earth. It is not perfectly identical everywhere, but it is stable enough to be useful.

gravity near Earth
g ≈ 9.81 m/s²

One way to imagine the discovery is simple: measure the object, drop it, measure the time, repeat. Different objects reveal the same pattern. Gravity keeps pulling.

classical chain
force→acceleration→velocity and position

Because gravity keeps acting every second, the apple keeps gaining speed.

velocity
velocity = initial velocity − (gravity × time)

And because velocity changes continuously, position changes faster and faster.

position
position = initial position + (initial velocity × time) − ½(gravity × time²)
falling apple · height over time
heighttime →

The time² part matters. The apple is not moving at the same speed. It is accelerating.

I liked formulas. I remember calculating things only out of curiosity: how fast something falls, how far it moves, how the numbers evolve.

05 · mathematics

Then mathematics opened another world.

I later studied math in college mostly out of curiosity about dynamics.

Probability. Calculus. Differential equations. Imaginary numbers.

Imaginary numbers felt crazy at first. Limits felt different: the idea that when calculating the exact number is hard, something can become so close to another value that, for practical purposes, we treat it as the same.

At first prediction looked simple:

measure variables→apply equations→predict outcome

But that simplicity does not survive every system.

06 · complexity

Then things become difficult again.

Weather. Traffic. Markets.

Not because the laws disappeared. Because the number of interactions becomes enormous.

This is the bridge to chaos. Lorenz helped make this idea famous with weather: small differences in starting conditions can grow into very different outcomes.

Another powerful example is gravity again.

Two planets orbiting each other can remain stable for long periods. Add a third object and tiny differences start growing with time.

chaos intuition
known rules + small errors + time = fragile prediction

The rules still work. Gravity still works. The equations still work.

Prediction is the part that breaks.

07 · fluids and quantum mechanics

Then physics becomes strange again.

Fluid mechanics gives a useful bridge. Air and water follow equations, but turbulence makes prediction difficult. The rules are there. The system becomes too sensitive.

At atomic scales, classical physics becomes even less intuitive. The analysis changes again.

The equations stop predicting one exact outcome and start predicting probabilities.

quantum mechanics
P(x) = |ψ(x)|²

In classical physics:

one system → one trajectory → one expected result.

In quantum mechanics:

one system → many possible outcomes → different probabilities.

Probability is no longer only a limitation of human knowledge.

It becomes part of the structure of reality itself.

Prediction begins with certainty.

Then approximation.

Then sensitivity.

Then probability.
