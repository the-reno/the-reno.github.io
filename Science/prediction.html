The Complexity
of Prediction.

In my view, prediction is one of the most interesting ideas in science. At first it looks like a problem of attention. Later it becomes a problem of probability, measurement, and limits.

01 · dice

I used to think prediction was repetition.

You roll a dice.

Six.

You roll it again in what feels like the same way.

Same hand. Same direction. Same force.

Three.

You roll it again.

Different again.

At first that felt strange to me. I thought repeating the movement should repeat the outcome.

Later I discovered a better strategy. Sometimes you cannot predict the exact result. But you can predict probabilities.

02 · probability

The randomness had structure.

A dice is simple. Six sides. Stable rules. Same probabilities.

The probability of rolling a specific number is known. Predictable.

basic probability
probability = successful possibilities ÷ all possibilities
one dice
probability of rolling 3 = 1 ÷ 6

I liked that immediately. The randomness still existed. But the randomness had structure.

03 · two dice

Some outcomes become more likely.

Take two dice. Now we are no longer trying to predict each dice individually. We are predicting the sum.

The number 7 appears often because many combinations create it:

1 + 62 + 53 + 44 + 35 + 26 + 1
two dice
probability of rolling 7 = 6 ÷ 36

But 2 is rare. Only one combination creates it: 1 + 1.

rare outcome
probability of rolling 2 = 1 ÷ 36
Even simple systems create patterns.
04 · gravity

Then physics enters the conversation.

An apple falling from a tree is also predictable.

Gravity pulls it down continuously. And gravity on Earth is extremely stable.

That word matters: stable.

near earth
gravity on Earth ≈ 9.81 meters per second²

That means people measured how objects accelerate near Earth. The value is almost always the same.

Because the force keeps acting every second, the apple keeps gaining speed.

velocity
velocity now = starting velocity − gravity × time

The interesting part for me is that the movement accelerates. The same force keeps acting through time.

position
position now = starting position + starting velocity × time − ½ × gravity × time²

The time² part is fascinating. The object is not only moving. Its speed is continuously changing while gravity keeps acting.

05 · classical physics

Known variables make prediction possible.

For most things we see every day, the same physical laws work surprisingly well across different scales.

Planets.Apples.A grain of sand.

The size changes. The rules mostly stay the same.

And we can keep measuring because the variables are known and relatively stable.

Position.Velocity.Gravity.Time.

Because those variables are measurable, prediction becomes possible.

That is classical physics. Newton.

06 · quantum

Then came the surprise.

For a long time, scientists believed this logic would continue forever.

Smaller object. Same rules.

But at atomic scales, classical physics stops being enough.

That is where quantum mechanics appears.

The important shift is simple. The equations stop predicting one exact outcome. Instead, they predict probabilities of possible outcomes.

quantum probability
probability of finding the particle = |wave function|²
classical physics
The object has a defined position. The equation tries to calculate it.
quantum mechanics
The equation itself becomes probabilistic. It gives possible outcomes.

That is very different from predicting the trajectory of a falling apple.

07 · complexity

Prediction breaks again at larger scales.

Weather. Traffic. Markets.

Not because the laws disappeared. But because the number of interactions becomes enormous.

Tiny differences at the beginning slowly grow with time.

Now imagine two planets orbiting each other.

The variables are known: mass, velocity, distance, gravity.

The motion becomes relatively predictable. Two planets can move in stable paths for very long periods.

But add a third object.

Everything changes.

Prediction becomes dramatically harder.

Not because gravity failed. Not because the equations disappeared.

The rules still work. Gravity still works. The equations still work.

Prediction is the part that breaks.

And that is the part that keeps pulling me back.
