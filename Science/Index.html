The jam that came from nowhere.

You waited twenty minutes for the accident. The road opened up and there was nothing there. No crash. No closure. Just normal traffic on the other side, like the jam had imagined itself.

It is the simplest possible thing. One driver brakes. The next driver brakes a little harder. And so on, all the way back. Watch it happen below.

Try it. Tap the brake button while traffic is flowing. One car stops for a second. Then it moves again. But the wave it created keeps going, backward through the line, long after that car is fine.

The wave seen from above.

Each row of pixels below is one moment in time. Time flows downward. Position along the road runs left to right. Red bands are jams. Watch them lean to the left as they move down. That tilt means the jam is traveling backward through traffic at about 15 km/h, while every car is moving forward at 90.

This was first studied in 2008 with 22 real cars driving on a circular track at 30 km/h. They were told to keep a constant speed. Within a few minutes, jams formed by themselves. No driver did anything wrong. The flow itself was unstable.

Why the wave does not dissipate.

Think of the cars as a row of springs. When one compresses, the next one compresses harder, then the next. If everyone were a perfect driver with infinite anticipation, the wave would dampen out. Real drivers react slightly late, slightly too hard, and the wave amplifies instead.

Above a critical density of cars per km, the math says small perturbations grow without bound. Below it, they fade. The dice roll, the storm, the jam — the same exponential mechanism shows up in all of them.

What to try.

Some things worth poking at:

Set cars to 10. Tap brake. The wave dies out.
Set cars to 25. Wait. A jam forms on its own.
Set cars to 20. Tap brake. The wave keeps going.
