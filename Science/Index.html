The jam that came from nowhere.

You waited twenty minutes for the accident. The road opened up and there was nothing there. No crash. No closure. Just normal traffic on the other side, like the jam had imagined itself.

It is the simplest possible thing. One driver brakes. The next driver brakes a little harder. And so on, all the way back. Watch it from above.

The wave seen across time.

Each row of pixels below is one moment in time. Time flows downward. Position along the road runs left to right. Red bands are jams averaged across all three lanes. Watch the diagonal stripes lean to the left as they move down. That tilt means the jam is traveling backward through traffic at about 15 km/h, while every car is moving forward.

This was first studied in 2008 with 22 real cars driving on a circular track. They were told to keep a constant speed. Within minutes, jams formed by themselves. No driver did anything wrong. The flow itself was unstable.

Why the wave does not dissipate.

Cars behave like a row of springs. When one compresses, the next one compresses harder, then the next. If everyone were a perfect driver with infinite anticipation, the wave would dampen out. Real drivers react slightly late, slightly too hard, and the wave amplifies instead.

Lane changes make it worse. Every time a car cuts in front of another, the one behind has to brake. That brake propagates backward in that lane, while the new lane the cutter joined has its own ripple from the gap they left. One weave creates two waves.

Above a critical density, the math says small perturbations grow without bound. Below it, they fade.

What to try.

Set cars to 8. Tap brake. Wave dies out fast.
Set cars to 20. Tap brake. Wave persists for minutes.
Set cars to 22. Force a lane change. Two waves form.
Set cars to 25. Wait. Jams form on their own.
